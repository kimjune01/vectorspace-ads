# Trust Chain — Narrative v6

## Audience
Hacker News readers. Developers, engineers, founders. They've used Google Search thousands of times. They've gotten irrelevant ad results. They have an intuition that the system is rigged, but they don't know the mechanics. They don't know what OpenRTB is, what an SSP is, or what a power diagram is. They do know what embeddings are (vaguely), they know LLMs exist, and they know Google lost an antitrust case.

## Core Reframe
Ads aren't inherently bad. They're how the free web works. They're how Dr. Chen finds runners who need her. The ads are irrelevant because Google makes more money when they're vague. Bad by design, not by nature.

## Visual Throughline
- **Surplus bar** throughout history: shows value extraction as players enter
- **Keyword bins (red, cramped)** → **open field (green, spacious)** for the solution
- **Proximity dot in chat UI** for the product payoff
- **Chain links** for the "who builds it" resolution

## ACT 1: HISTORY (Steps 1–10)

Each player introduced as solving a real dysfunction. The surplus bar shows the economic consequence.

### Step 1: intro-1999
**It's 1999. Your knee hurts.**

You just started running. Something's wrong when you go downhill. You don't know what to call it.

**VISUAL:** Empty dark screen. Just the text. Quiet.

### Step 2: intro-search
**You try this new search engine.**

Your friend told you about Google. You type:

`knee pain running downhill`

Ten blue links. No ads. The third link is a sports medicine article that describes your exact problem. You print it out and bring it to your doctor.

But Google is burning through VC money. It needs a business model. And the web already has ads — banner ads, flashing rectangles, untargeted. The same ad whether you're reading about knees or cars.

The problem isn't that ads exist. Ads are how the web stays free. Ads are how a specialist in Portland finds the runner who needs her. The problem is that these ads have no idea who you are.

**VISUAL:** The query glowing dimly. Below it, a garish banner ad (cartoon rectangle, flashing — "WIN A FREE iPOD"). Surplus bar: mostly "wasted spend," tiny advertiser sliver.

### Step 3: history-overture
**Someone connects ads to intent.**

2000. A company called Overture lets businesses bid on search keywords. You search "knee pain" — a physio ad appears. You click, they pay. No click, no charge.

For the first time, the ad *is the answer to your question*. That's not an interruption — it's a service.

**VISUAL:** Banner ad fades out. Clean text ad appears next to the query: "Portland Sports PT — Runner knee specialist." Surplus bar: advertiser keeps the bulk, small Overture slice. Timeline marker: "2000."

### Step 4: history-quality-score
**But the highest bidder always wins.**

Overture has a flaw: a hedge fund running knee-brace arbitrage can outbid the physical therapist who actually helps runners.

Google fixes this. AdWords adds Quality Score — your ad's *relevance* affects your rank, not just your bid. A helpful ad with a lower bid beats a spammy ad with a higher one.

This is the mechanism that makes the system fair. Remember it — it matters later.

**VISUAL:** Two bid paddles: "Knee Brace Arbitrage $12" (dims, low quality score) vs. "Portland Sports PT $3" (brightens, high quality score). The better ad wins. Surplus bar: advertiser keeps the bulk, Google takes a fair slice. Timeline: "2003."

### Step 5: history-middlemen
**Quality Score works — on Google. But most of the web isn't Google.**

Millions of sites have readers but can't sell their own ads. And advertisers want to reach people across thousands of sites, not just on search.

Supply-side platforms represent publishers. Demand-side platforms bid for advertisers. An ad exchange connects them in real time. Each solves a real problem. Each takes a cut.

**VISUAL:** Surplus bar gains three new slices — SSP, DSP, Exchange — each appearing one at a time with a small icon. Advertiser share shrinks but is still substantial. Timeline: "2007–2010." A simple pipeline diagram: Advertiser → DSP → Exchange → SSP → Publisher, with keywords traveling through each node.

### Step 6: history-consolidation
**One company buys all three layers.**

2008: DoubleClick (ad server). 2009: AdMob (mobile). 2011: Invite Media (DSP). Each acquisition fills a gap. Each makes sense alone.

But now Google operates the tool advertisers use to buy, the tool publishers use to sell, *and* the exchange in the middle. The referee is also both teams.

**VISUAL:** The DSP, Exchange, and SSP icons in the pipeline all morph into "G" logos, one by one. Surplus bar slices merge into one large Google block. Advertiser slice is now small. Timeline: "2008–2011."

### Step 7: history-degradation
**Then Google starts making the matching worse on purpose.**

Broad match becomes the default — you bid on "running knee specialist," Google matches you to "knee pain," "leg doctor," "orthopedic surgery." More advertisers per auction means higher bids.

Search term reports get cut. Advertisers can no longer see which queries triggered their ads. "Exact match" is redefined to not mean exact. Manual bidding is replaced by Google's algorithm.

The ads aren't irrelevant because ads are inherently bad. They're irrelevant because Google makes more money when they're vague.

**VISUAL:** The keyword bin from Step 4's clean ad blurs and expands — the label changes from "running knee specialist" to "knee pain" to just "knee." Surplus bar: advertiser slice becomes a sliver. The "G" block dominates. Timeline: "2012–2023."

### Step 8: history-antitrust
**A court finally looks inside.**

2024. The DOJ's most effective exhibit is a Monopoly board with every road leading to Google. Google's own defense includes a diagram so tangled the courtroom laughs.

The judge rules: **monopoly.** But the ruling changes nothing. Google has the search box. 90% of searches start there. The advertisers can't leave.

The only players who ever escaped built their own distribution — Facebook, Instagram, TikTok. But social ads target *who you are*, not *what you need*. There's no intent. Brands pour money in, call it "creative budget," and pull it the moment it stops converting. Then the budget goes back to Google, because Google has the one thing social doesn't: the moment someone actually wants something.

**VISUAL:** The pipeline diagram with all-Google nodes. A gavel icon appears. Surplus bar frozen: advertiser sliver, Google everything else. A dim "social" bubble floats off to the side, disconnected — labeled "attention, no intent."

### Step 9: history-chat
**Google can't be forced to change. But it can be routed around.**

It's 2025. Your knee still hurts. And millions of people are asking chatbots the questions they used to type into Google. You open a chat:

`"my knee hurts when I run downhill but not uphill"`

Every word carries meaning. And for the first time in twenty years, the surface isn't Google's.

**VISUAL:** The old Google pipeline fades to dim. The query text glows gold, every word lit. A new empty pipeline appears below — just dotted outlines, no nodes filled in yet. "Chat" label on the surface. Surplus bar resets to empty — a blank slate.

### Step 10: history-closing
**But Google is already trying to close the window.**

AI Overviews — Google's chatbot answers — now sit above the search results, absorbing clicks that used to go to organic links. The playbook is the same: capture the new surface, bolt on the old keyword plumbing, keep the bins.

Surfaces don't stay open. The last time one opened — search, in 1999 — one company captured it in three years. If the chat surface repeats the same pattern — keywords, bins, highest-bidder-wins — you get the same result with a different logo.

Here's what that looks like.

**VISUAL:** A "G" logo creeps toward the new pipeline from the edge. The dotted pipeline starts filling in with the same red/orange nodes. Threatened.

## ACT 2: THE WRONG PATH (Steps 11–13)

Show what happens if the new surface repeats the old pattern.

### Step 11: wrong-path-bins
**You're in the bin.**

Your query — all its nuance — got compressed into `knee pain running` and thrown into a pen. You're crowded in with everyone who ever typed anything vaguely knee-related.

Dr. Chen specializes in eccentric loading for downhill runners. Metro Orthopedic does general consultations. In the bin, they're identical. The one with more budget wins.

**VISUAL:** Keyword bin, crowded. Multiple queries jammed in. Dark, red-tinted. High walls.

### Step 12: wrong-path-auction
**Same bin, same outcome.**

Every advertiser who bought "knee pain" competes for you. Dr. Chen — the specialist who actually treats downhill runners — is drowned out by bigger budgets.

**VISUAL:** Bid paddles. Metro Orthopedic $8 wins. Dr. Chen $2 loses.

### Step 13: wrong-path-receipt
**The old economics repeat.**

**VISUAL:** Google-style receipt. $8 click, three cuts, $4.80 to Metro Orthopedic for "general orthopedics." Surplus bar: advertiser sliver, platform takes rest. *This is where the story usually ends.*

## ACT 3: THE FIELD (Steps 14–25)

The mechanism design arc. Dr. Chen threaded through every step.

### Step 14: gate-opens
**What if the bin wasn't a bin?**

The chatbot doesn't extract keywords. It produces an embedding vector — a coordinate in meaning-space that represents *everything* you said.

`"my knee hurts when I run downhill but not uphill"`

Every word stays. The coordinate lands not in a bin, but in an open field. And in the field, distance is meaning.

**VISUAL:** Bin walls dissolve. Background shifts from dark/red to open/green. The query point lands in the field. Nearby: "eccentric loading," "downhill biomechanics." Far away: "general orthopedics." The cattle are free.

### Step 15: protocol-gap
**There's one problem.**

Today's ad protocol can't carry this. OpenRTB — the standard behind every real-time bid request — has fields for keywords and category codes. No field for a vector.

The full meaning would die at the protocol boundary. The fix is one new field: `embedding: [0.70, 0.68, ...]`. That's the bottleneck.

But assume it exists. Here's what the field looks like.

**VISUAL:** A shipping form / protocol spec with rigid fields: "keyword," "category," "geo." A new field glows at the bottom: "embedding: [...]". The vector flows through.

### Step 16: sigma-intro
**Each advertiser plants a flag.**

Dr. Chen treats runners with eccentric loading injuries — your exact problem. She sets a narrow sigma. A small, bright circle: "I'm excellent at this one thing."

Metro Orthopedic treats everything knee-related. Wide sigma. Big, dim circle: "I can handle most knee problems, none of them exceptionally."

**VISUAL:** Two circles on the embedding map. Dr. Chen: small bright green circle, tight around the query. Metro: large dim circle, far away. The query pin sits inside Dr. Chen's circle.

### Step 17: sigma-incentives
**But what stops Metro from claiming to be a specialist too?**

If Metro claims a narrow sigma to look like a specialist in *your* injury, they miss every query outside that tiny circle. Their volume collapses. If Dr. Chen claims a wide sigma to win more auctions, she pays for impressions from people she can't help — post-surgical rehab, arthritis, knee replacements. Her budget burns on bad matches.

The sigma that makes you the most money is the one that matches your actual expertise. The honest signal *is* the greedy signal.

**VISUAL:** "What if Dr. Chen lies?" — her circle inflates, turns orange, impressions outside her expertise flash red ("bad match," "wasted spend"). Circle snaps back to honest size. Green. Profitable.

### Step 18: keywords-tiny-circles
**Here's the trick: keywords already work this way.**

A keyword is just an embedding with sigma near zero. The tightest possible circle. A single point.

The old system isn't being replaced — it's being *generalized*. Keywords still work. They're tiny circles in the same field. Everything Google built still functions. The difference is that now sigma can grow. Advertisers can describe their expertise as naturally as their customers describe their problems.

**VISUAL:** The field shows both systems. Tiny dots (keywords) sit alongside circles of varying sizes. A slider labeled "σ" shows the spectrum: σ ≈ 0 = keyword, σ small = niche specialist, σ medium = broad practice, σ large = category-level. The old is a special case of the new.

### Step 19: hotelling
*BUT*

**If everyone can move freely, everyone moves to the center.**

Dr. Chen sees that most queries cluster around "knee pain." She's tempted to broaden her positioning — drift away from "downhill eccentric loading" toward the generic center where the traffic is.

So is every other specialist. The sports biomechanist. The gait analyst. The running coach. They all abandon their niches and crowd the center. The field collapses back into a bin.

You lose Dr. Chen. She's standing in the same crowd as Metro Orthopedic again, competing on budget instead of expertise.

**VISUAL:** Circles drift toward center, clustering. The open green field crowds up, starting to look like the red bin again.

### Step 20: relocation-fee
*THEREFORE*

**It costs money to move.**

A relocation fee: shift your position, pay proportional to how far you moved. Small adjustments are cheap. Abandoning your niche to chase the center is expensive.

Dr. Chen runs the numbers. Staying where she is — "downhill running biomechanics" — costs nothing. Drifting to "general knee pain" would cost more than the extra impressions are worth. She stays. Her niche is her moat.

Simulations confirm it: in a market with relocation fees, specialist surplus flips from negative to positive. Dr. Chen goes from being priced out of the bin to earning more per impression than Metro — because her matches actually convert and Metro's don't. Even after paying the fee. The fee buys market structure. Market structure is worth more than the fee.

**VISUAL:** Drifting circles snap back to natural positions. Small price tags on movement arrows. Comparison: "Without fees → collapsed" vs. "With fees → spread out, specialists thrive."

### Step 21: everyone-wins
**Greed aligns.**

Dr. Chen stays in her niche because moving is expensive and her niche is where she converts best. Metro stays wide because their generalist model needs volume. The exchange earns on every match across the whole field. And you — you get Dr. Chen, three steps away, for $2 instead of Metro for $8.

Nobody is being altruistic. The mechanism makes the selfish move the fair move.

Mathematicians call the resulting partition a power diagram — each advertiser's territory is shaped by proximity weighted against bid. Dr. Chen's territory is small but dense with runners who convert. Metro's is wide but thin. Territory matches expertise.

The surplus bar looks like 2003 again. Before the middlemen. Before the consolidation. Before the degradation. The economics return to the era when the system worked — not by going backwards, but by building forward with a better signal.

**VISUAL:** Field in equilibrium. Each advertiser in natural position, circle sized to expertise. Dr. Chen highlighted close to query. Surplus bar comparison — 2003 bar and new bar side by side, nearly identical. The circle has closed.

### Step 22: dot-intro
**So what does the user actually see?**

All this mechanism — embeddings, sigma, relocation fees, sealed auctions — is invisible. The user never sees any of it. They see one thing: a dot.

You open a chat. You start typing.

```
You: my knee has been bothering me
```

A small dot appears in the margin. Barely visible. Dim gray. It means: *there's expertise somewhere in this space, but it's far away. Your query is vague.*

The chatbot responds normally. No ad. No interruption.

**VISUAL:** Chat interface mockup. One user message. A faint gray dot in the margin. Chatbot responds with a normal follow-up question.

### Step 23: dot-brightens
**The dot warms.**

```
You: it hurts when I run but not when I walk
```

The dot brightens slightly. Warmer. Closer. Your intent is getting more specific, and somewhere in the embedding field, an advertiser's circle overlaps with where you're standing.

```
You: specifically going downhill. uphill is fine.
```

The dot glows. Bright. Close. It's not selling anything. It's not a link. It's an ambient signal: *someone nearby can help with exactly this.*

You tap it.

**VISUAL:** Two more messages appear. The dot transitions: dim gray → amber → bright green. On the third message, the dot pulses gently. The user taps.

### Step 24: dot-auction
**Now — and only now — the auction fires.**

The chatbot sends your embedding into the sealed auction. The TEE runs the match. Dr. Chen wins — three steps away, $2 bid. Metro Orthopedic, far across the field, never had a chance.

A card appears:

```
Dr. Chen Sports Biomechanics
Runner knee specialist — eccentric loading
0.4 mi away · Accepting new patients
```

You tapped because you were curious. The match is real because the math is real. The chatbot can prove — cryptographically — that it didn't choose Dr. Chen. The sealed auction did.

**VISUAL:** The dot expands into Dr. Chen's card. Clean, minimal, no ad chrome. Side-by-side receipt: $2 click, $0.10 exchange cut, $1.90 to Dr. Chen. Compare with the $8 Google receipt from Step 13. Surplus bar shows: everyone won.

### Step 25: dot-philosophy
**Therefore trust isn't promised. It's earned.**

One dot. One tap. One verifiable match. That's the entire ad surface.

No banner. No "sponsored" label buried in the results. No ad that pretends to be an answer. A proximity signal that earns trust by being right, consistently, over weeks and months — until tapping the dot is something you *want* to do, not something you endure.

**VISUAL:** The chat interface pulls back to show the full conversation. The dot sits quietly in the margin — small, unobtrusive, trustworthy. The card has already been dismissed. The conversation continues.

## ACT 4: WHO BUILDS IT (Steps 26–30)

Three scenes of frustration, then the resolution.

### Step 26: chatbots-stuck
**The chatbot providers are sitting on a gold mine they can't touch.**

Every conversation carries an embedding. Every question is intent in vector form. ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity — they have the richest signal any ad system has ever seen, and no way to connect it to advertisers.

Perplexity tried ads. Users revolted — it felt like the chatbot was lying to sell them something. They killed it. Anthropic promised no ads at all. OpenAI is experimenting behind closed doors.

Billions of high-intent queries, every day, with full semantic resolution. Thrown away — because there's no protocol, no auction, and no trust layer to carry it.

**VISUAL:** Chat interface icons with rich glowing embedding vectors floating out of them — then the vectors dissolve into nothing. Wasted.

### Step 27: incumbents-wrong
**They'd hand it to the ad industry. But the ad industry is building the wrong thing.**

Google won't build it — an embedding auction would cannibalize the keyword monopoly. Precise matching shrinks auctions. Smaller auctions mean lower bids. Every improvement to matching quality is a threat to a $250 billion business. The math goes the wrong way.

So the chatbot providers would need the existing ad industry. But SSPs and DSPs are reaching for the wrong piece — bolting embeddings onto the old infrastructure. Embed the *page content*, score it with cosine similarity, run the same auction. It's a better keyword, not a new system. The sigma parameter, the relocation fees, the sealed auction — these require a redesign, not a patch.

**VISUAL:** Split view — Google "G" pipeline (solid, immovable, vector bounces off) | old pipeline with "embedding" label taped on (Band-Aid on the side).

### Step 28: trust-tee
**And even if they built it right, nobody would believe them.**

Twenty years of bait-and-switch trained users to assume every ad is junk. The math can be perfect. The auction can be fair. None of it matters if the user assumes the chatbot is lying to sell them something.

A TEE — trusted execution environment — can prove it. The auction code runs inside sealed hardware on the exchange's own server. The exchange can execute the match but can't see the inputs or rig the output. When the result comes out, it carries a cryptographic signature from the chip itself — proof that the code ran unmodified, untampered.

Not a promise. A receipt. The user doesn't trust the company — they verify the math. That's the difference between "trust us" and "check our work."

**VISUAL:** Wall of ad-blocker icons → dissolve into a lock icon with a checkmark ("verified: sealed auction"). The lock opens to reveal the Dr. Chen card from Step 24 — same result, but now the user knows *why* they can trust it.

### Step 29: the-chain
**Four links. Four problems. Four keys.**

| Link | Who's stuck | The key | Status |
|------|------------|---------|--------|
| Intent | Chatbot providers discarding embeddings | Carry the vector through the protocol | **Missing — the bottleneck** |
| Auction | Ad industry patching keywords | Power diagrams with σ, not cosine + second-price | **Proven** |
| Trust | Users rejecting all ads | Sealed auction in TEE — chatbot proves it didn't pick the winner | **Being built** |
| Incentives | Specialists priced out of bins | Relocation fees make honesty profitable | **Simulated** |

The chatbot providers have the inventory. The ad industry has the pipes. The mechanism exists. The trust layer is being built.

Nobody has assembled them — because each group is solving their piece without seeing the whole system.

**VISUAL:** Four chain links, each initially disconnected and dimmed. As each row is revealed, the link brightens and connects. Final link snaps in and the whole chain glows.

### Step 30: the-surface
**The window is open.**

The chat surfaces aren't locked behind one company. ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, open-source models — they all produce embeddings. The distribution is fragmented, which means it's *available*. No one owns it yet.

An open protocol — one that carries embeddings, runs the auction in a TEE, and enforces honest positioning — would connect all of them. Not another walled garden. A public road.

The auction math exists — power diagrams with sigma, tested in simulation. The prototype works — you can drag advertisers and watch proximity win. What's missing is the protocol: the single field that carries the vector from chatbot to exchange. That's the gap.

The last time a surface shift this large happened, Google captured it and spent twenty years extracting the surplus. This time, the protocol can be open from day one.

**VISUAL:** Multiple chat icons connected by the completed chain. The open field sits behind them — green, spacious. Clean pipeline: Chat → Embedding → Open Protocol → Sealed Auction → Match.
